# **Executive Summary** {#executive-summary}

**Despite their central political and economic role, social media platforms have increasingly retreated into obscurity**. The early “golden age” of platform transparency—when open and flexible data access enabled thousands of independent research projects—has given way to a landscape defined by opacity. Data transparency in social media is fundamental because it enables independent scrutiny, evidence-based policymaking, and democratic accountability in systems that shape public discourse at scale. Yet most user-generated content and advertising data are currently largely inaccessible to researchers. 

**The 2025 Social Media Data Transparency Index reveals a deepening crisis in social media data access that directly undermines not only public-interest research but also democratic oversight and the credibility of digital advertising**. Our systematic assessment of 15 major platforms across the European Union, the United Kingdom, and Brazil exposes a two-tier transparency regime: few platforms offer limited access in select regulatory environments, while the vast majority remain effectively opaque.

**The report reveals that numbers from digital advertising, which lies at the core of social media companies’ business models, are largely unauditable.** While the lack of transparency in user-generated (UGC) content has been widely debated, access to advertising content is even more constrained and receives less attention. In our assessments of advertising data, no platform achieved strong ratings. When available, data are highly aggregated and insufficient for analyzing targeting practices, reach, or societal impact. Even the best-performing platforms, such as Meta and YouTube, offer narrowly searchable ad archives, only in the EU due to regulatory constraints. These archives, however, have restrictions on bulk access, historical depth, and analytical functionality.

**With few exceptions, access to User-Generated Content (UGC) data is either nonexistent or deficient.** Several widely used platforms—Discord, Pinterest, Snapchat, Kwai (also known as Kuaishou or Snack Video), Telegram, and Reddit—offer no transparency mechanisms for user-generated content or advertising data, while most major platforms, such as Meta’s services, LinkedIn, and TikTok, provide at best only limited access. Meta’s replacement of CrowdTangle with the highly restrictive Meta Content Library has sharply reduced meaningful researcher access, and currently, only Bluesky and YouTube offer comparatively stronger transparency for user-generated content, albeit with limitations.

**Policymakers should restore the observability of user-generated content and introduce robust frameworks to ensure advertising transparency.** After a few years of relative openness, platforms are now trending toward opacity and regional asymmetries. Reverting this requires legal provisions that allow the collection of publicly available data for public-interest research, while strengthening international cooperation to standardize interoperable data access protocols and reduce global inequalities. 

# **Recommendations** {#recommendations}

## *Global Governance* {#global-governance}

* **Support the development of interoperable standards and protocols for data transparency on social media platforms.** International bodies responsible for defining technical and policy standards for online communication should actively support the development and adoption of interoperable, harmonised standards and protocols for the collection and analysis of UGC and advertising data on social media. These standards should aim to reduce the current fragmentation of access mechanisms and tools, enabling consistent cross-platform research.

* **Ensure observability by using standardized, clearly defined criteria.** Policymakers must work to open new legal avenues to ensure platform transparency and data access. New bills and regulatory proposals should be guided by global principles for information integrity. This approach would enable standardized access to high-quality platform data, reducing unilateral decision-making that directly affects the feasibility of in-depth research and investigations.

* **Reassure researchers about the ethical use of scraping.** Regulators should explicitly clarify that web scraping of public UGC and ad data for public interest research is lawful. Clear guidance should be issued on data collection best practices, aligned with data protection and ethical standards, to reduce legal uncertainty and chilling effects.

  ## *Regional Policy-making*  {#regional-policy-making}

* **European Union: Clarify and fully implement Article 40 of the Digital Services Act.** The remit of Article 40(12) should be clarified to avoid restrictive interpretations that undermine its purpose. Article 40 should be implemented in its entirety, guaranteeing vetted researchers full, timely, and continuous access to relevant platform data, including advertising repositories, content dissemination metrics, and systemic risk indicators.

* **UK: Create a data access framework that guarantees access to social media data on UGC and advertising for public-interest research and oversight.** The UK should introduce legal provisions that explicitly guarantee researchers’ ability to collect publicly available data via scraping and other automated means for public-interest research, in compliance with the UK GDPR and the Online Safety Act 2023\.

* **Brazil: Create a data access framework that guarantees access to social media data on UGC and advertising for public-interest research and oversight.** Brazil should adopt a data access framework, either through a new data access bill or by updating existing legislation, to enhance the transparency and accountability of social media data. The new framework should be aligned with existing data and consumer protection laws and can focus solely on data transparency aspects, while building on prior proposals, such as the law bill PL 2630/2020.

  ## *Social Media Companies* {#social-media-companies}

* **Harmonise data access practices to the highest global standard.** Social media companies should align their global data access practices with the most transparent and accessible regulatory regimes, rather than applying minimal or jurisdiction-specific compliance. This would help prevent the reinforcement of global inequalities in research capacity, oversight, and accountability between regions.

* **Offer comprehensive and granular access to advertisement data.** Platforms should implement or significantly improve transparency reporting practices by moving beyond high-level summaries and providing disaggregated, methodologically transparent information on systemic risks, content moderation, advertising practices, and enforcement outcomes. Transparency reports should include clear definitions, methodological explanations, and access to underlying datasets to enable independent scrutiny, replication of findings, and cross-platform comparison.

* **Provide reliable, stable, and extensive API access for researchers conducting public-interest research.** Platforms should provide stable, well-documented, and comprehensive API access for academic and vetted researchers conducting public-interest research. Priority should be given to research on systemic risks, including coordinated disinformation, political advertising, harassment and abuse, algorithmic amplification, and other forms of platform manipulation. Access conditions should ensure continuity, reproducibility, and independent validation, while incorporating appropriate privacy and security safeguards.

* **Establish secure and functional data sandboxes.** Platforms should create data sandboxes that allow researchers to access sensitive or high-risk datasets (e.g, coordinated behaviour, systemic risks, or integrity enforcement) under controlled conditions. These environments should enable independent verification of claims made in transparency reports and public communications, rather than limiting access to pre-aggregated or non-exportable outputs.

* **Enable data exploration and extraction through user-friendly interfaces.** Platforms should enable data exploration and extraction through accessible graphical user interfaces in order to democratise accountability and oversight. Tools with advanced search, filtering, and export functionalities would allow non-experts and civil society actors to meaningfully participate in accountability processes. These interfaces should provide comprehensive access to public UGC and advertising data, including the ability to download datasets and conduct analysis outside restrictive virtual environments. Access should not be arbitrarily constrained by popularity, engagement metrics, or opaque eligibility criteria, and should include historical data where technically feasible.

  ## *Public Interest Researchers*  {#public-interest-researchers}

* **Advance research-driven advertising transparency.** Researchers should use the available methods to collect and analyse digital advertising data. Where platform reporting is incomplete or inconsistent, researchers should document gaps and push for comprehensive advertising transparency, including access to ad creatives, targeting parameters, delivery metrics, funding sources, and cross-platform dissemination patterns.

* **Develop alternative monitoring strategies for zero-access platforms.** In contexts where platforms deny or severely restrict data access, researchers should invest in complementary monitoring approaches, such as panel studies, browser-based measurement tools, or collaborative data donation projects, while transparently documenting limitations and risks.

* **Support and engage with global transparency frameworks.** Researchers should actively support global regulatory and standard-setting efforts that create durable incentives for transparency, interoperability, and accountability. This includes contributing technical expertise, documenting access failures, and collaborating across regions to strengthen the evidence base for platform governance.

# **Key Findings** {#key-findings}

**The 2025 Social Media Data Transparency Index reveals a fragmented, largely opaque landscape of data access, marked by significant disparities across platforms, regions, and data types.** We developed a systematic framework to evaluate social media platforms’ user-generated content and advertising data, with an emphasis on transparency and accountability. Drawing on public-interest principles and technological best practices, we applied this framework to assess social media platforms across three regions (the European Union, the United Kingdom, and Brazil). 

**Our results show that most of the 15 social media platforms assessed offer minimal to no transparency.** Widely popular platforms such as Discord, Pinterest, Snapchat, and Kwai provide no transparency mechanisms in any of the assessed regions for either ads or UGC. Additionally, Meta’s current data access policies don’t permit structured data extraction, severely hindering research into its Instagram, Facebook, WhatsApp, and Threads platforms. 

**While regulatory frameworks are being rolled out in the EU, much of the world still lags far behind on data transparency**. Platform evaluations in the EU achieve the highest average scores, with the UK close behind. In other regions, however, the gap is alarming, as illustrated by Brazil’s evaluations on advertisement data transparency, which is already weaker than UGC transparency across all regions.

**Asymmetries stemming from different local regulatory frameworks reinforce critical global inequalities**. Selective transparency, granted primarily in jurisdictions with robust regulatory oversight, produces significant disparities in knowledge production. This approach leaves much of the world in the dark, particularly in regions where social media data is most urgently needed to address human rights, democratic accountability, and socioeconomic challenges.

**User-Generated Content**

**With few exceptions, access to UGC data is either nonexistent or deficient.** Most platforms (namely, Kwai, Snapchat, Pinterest, Discord, Meta’s platforms, LinkedIn, and TikTok) were found to be deficient or worse in UGC data transparency. 

**The end of CrowdTangle has radically reduced access to UGC data on Meta’s platforms.** Crowdtangle, Meta’s previous solution for UGC data transparency, offered relatively flexible access to UGC content but was discontinued in 2024\. Meta replaced it with the Meta Content Library, a far more restricted and controlled environment that does not permit data extraction for independent research.

**Only Bluesky and YouTube provide free, programmatic, full-scope access to UGC, making them the top performers in our UGC transparency assessment.** Yet, they are not perfect. Bluesky’s open architecture supports broad access but is poorly documented, while YouTube’s well-documented API is constrained by quotas. By contrast, X monetizes data access heavily, while TikTok and LinkedIn limit full API access to approved researchers in the EU and the UK.

**Even on platforms with some level of transparency, critical bottlenecks hinder research into social media.** Native geographic or language-based filtering capabilities, which are useful for targeted research, are often absent from APIs. To conduct localised studies without these filters, researchers must extract large datasets and use computationally expensive techniques, which introduce sources of bias and error, require significant infrastructure, and disadvantage low-resourced researchers. Likewise, most platforms limit or prevent access to historical content, creating barriers to longitudinal studies, trend analysis, and retrospective investigations. 

**Advertising data**

**For advertising data access, the assessment reveals an even more troubling pattern than user-generated content.** While Bluesky and YouTube achieved “Strong” ratings for UGC transparency in the study, no platform reached this benchmark for advertising data. Instead, Kwai, Telegram, Discord, Pinterest, and Reddit have their business-advertising environments completely opaque to systematic, data-driven scrutiny, providing no advertising transparency whatsoever across all regions assessed. Across platforms, the European Union demonstrates markedly stronger transparency standards than Brazil and the United Kingdom. 

**All platforms provide only aggregated data, preventing meaningful, complex analysis of advertising outreach and impact.** The lack of granular and detailed data limits researchers’ ability to examine critical events, such as large-scale information operations or paid disinformation campaigns. Without such detail, it is not possible to assess who is being targeted, how widely advertisements are distributed, or how different user groups are affected, making it difficult to meaningfully evaluate the reach, influence, and potential societal impact of online advertising.

**This opacity distorts the advertising market.** When independent verification is not possible, marketing investment relies on platform metrics that cannot be properly audited, encouraging belief in “magical numbers.” The lack of access to granular, verifiable advertising data weakens confidence in the information integrity of the digital advertising environment.

# **Background** {#background}

## *Revisiting the “golden age” of platform transparency* {#revisiting-the-“golden-age”-of-platform-transparency}

As part of their efforts to build integrated web ecosystems around their services in the mid-2000s and early 2010s, online platforms allowed third-party developers to access and redistribute data at scale. In particular, this was made possible by social media platforms’ provision of public Application Programming Interfaces (APIs), following a period of rapid growth that consolidated their informational, social, and commercial importance, as well as their broader societal acceptance. This enabled social media expansion to be not only quantitative but also infrastructural, fostering a newly platformized web and consolidating platforms’ economic, social, and technological power.

Although not originally intended for this purpose, these data flows were quickly recognized by researchers as a valuable resource for meaningful social research. With basic programming skills, scholars were able to collect unprecedented volumes of real-time, non-sensitive data on social, political, and cultural phenomena with relative ease—at a scale far beyond what traditional methods such as surveys, interviews, and ethnography could achieve. 

This period of data openness, often described as the “golden age” of social media research, was short-lived. Its decline was driven largely by deep-seated problems in platform governance, particularly public revelations of the misuse of user data and widespread privacy violations. Between 2016 and 2018, following the Cambridge Analytica scandal, platforms adopted more restrictive data protection measures, discontinuing or severely limiting data collection and analysis tools previously used by researchers.

Platforms shape, mediate, and amplify users’ interactions, ideas, and access to information in complex and often opaque ways that do not necessarily align with users’ best interests. Independent access to social media platforms' data is thus vital, because such access enables researchers to ask socially consequential questions and to answer them rigorously. This includes supporting assessments of how platform products and services generate or amplify societal harms that disproportionately affect vulnerable and marginalized groups ([Dobber et al., 2023](https://doi.org/10.1177/14614448231157640); [Hendrix, 2025](https://www.techpolicy.press/why-independent-researchers-need-better-access-to-platform-data/); [Tromble, 2021](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/abs/10.1177/2056305121988929)). Given their substantial influence over public debate, information access, and freedom of expression, these platforms’ operations constitute matters of public interest, and therefore warrant public scrutiny and accountability.

Shifting towards opacity, platforms were not only concerned with addressing the privacy violations they had themselves enabled but also with curbing critical scholarly research that could increase reputational risk and invite regulatory scrutiny. As researchers increasingly relied on empirical data to expose deliberate disruptions to information integrity, they became a clear target of platform discontent.These disruptions undermined users’ ability to access accurate, consistent, and reliable information, free from hate, manipulation, and falsehoods, as well as key guarantees of freedom of expression and safety ([European Parliament, 2024](https://www.europarl.europa.eu/RegData/etudes/BRIE/2024/767153/EPRS_BRI\(2024\)767153_EN.pdf); [United Nations, 2024](https://www.un.org/sites/un2.un.org/files/un-global-principles-for-information-integrity-en.pdf)). They include electoral manipulation; the rise of hate speech targeting minorities and other social groups; terrorist and neo-Nazi recruitment and mobilization; coordinated disinformation campaigns; foreign influence operations; the amplification of conspiracy theories; the monetization of harmful and misleading content; and the large-scale dissemination of microtargeted scams causing financial and emotional harm. All of these phenomena operate within platform ecosystems at historically unprecedented levels of volume, velocity, and virality ([Benkler et al., 2018](https://academic.oup.com/book/26406); [Bradshaw & Howard, 2019](https://demtech.oii.ox.ac.uk/wp-content/uploads/sites/12/2019/09/CyberTroop-Report19.pdf); [Davey & Ebner, 2019](https://www.isdglobal.org/isd-publications/the-great-replacement-the-violent-consequences-of-mainstreamed-extremism/); [Marwick & Lewis, 2017](https://datasociety.net/library/media-manipulation-and-disinfo-online/); [Santini et al., 2025](https://netlab.eco.ufrj.br/en/post/transparency-under-threat-progress-and-setbacks-between-crowdtangle-and-the-meta-content-library); [Tucker et al., 2018](https://papers.ssrn.com/sol3/papers.cfm?abstract_id=3144139)).

Over the following years, platforms continued to introduce more limited and less reliable data access methods, while discontinuing other solutions that had previously been widely used. Twitter (now X), long regarded as one of the most research-friendly social media platforms in terms of data access and transparency, started charging users and researchers for data collection through its API in 2023 ([Hendrix, 2023](https://www.techpolicy.press/twitter-api-changes-set-to-disrupt-public-interest-research/)), with costs widely considered prohibitive for most research institutions worldwide ([Mashable, 2025](https://tech.yahoo.com/articles/x-api-costs-developers-42k-170210147.html)).

Another representative episode of this trend was Meta’s announcement of the discontinuation of CrowdTangle, a formerly independent research tool later acquired by the company, and its subsequent replacement with a tool offering substantially more limited reach and functionality ([Santini et al., 2025](https://netlab.eco.ufrj.br/en/post/transparency-under-threat-progress-and-setbacks-between-crowdtangle-and-the-meta-content-library)). In the meantime, some platforms promoted a narrow set of official data access solutions, such as the TikTok Research API, which is restricted to researchers based in the United States and the European Union and has been criticized for limitations in data completeness and reliability, effectively excluding much of the world ([Pearson et al., 2025](https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/full/10.1080/1369118X.2024.2420032)).

To make matters worse, large language models (LLMs) and other generative artificial intelligence (AI) systems increasingly rely on vast amounts of data derived from texts, images, and videos published on online platforms, which can hinder independent research. Social media companies—including Reddit, and X—have expressed strong resistance to the use of what they frame as “proprietary data” for training new models without compensation ([Mehta, 2025](https://techcrunch.com/2025/06/05/x-changes-its-terms-to-bar-training-of-ai-models-using-its-content/); [Shakir, 2023](https://www.theverge.com/2023/4/18/23688463/reddit-developer-api-terms-change-monetization-ai); [Tan, 2024](https://www.nytimes.com/2024/06/26/technology/terms-service-ai-training.html)). As a consequence, both permissioned and unpermissioned access to social media data by researchers has become increasingly difficult and contentious, as the same data access mechanisms developed for scholarly inquiry can also be leveraged by large AI companies to train commercial tools ([McGrady & Zuckerman, 2025](https://www.techpolicy.press/ai-companies-threaten-independent-social-media-research/)).

## *Legal Frameworks for UGC Data Access* {#legal-frameworks-for-ugc-data-access}

As regulatory frameworks aimed at limiting the power of large online platforms have come under increasing scrutiny worldwide, issues of transparency and access to public-interest data have gained prominence. Transparency requirements for user-generated content and platform data are now explicitly governed by recent digital regulatory frameworks across multiple jurisdictions

***The European Union’s Digital Services Act***

The European Union stands out as an example of particularly far-reaching and comprehensive regulation. In the EU, the Digital Services Act (DSA) establishes a comprehensive legal framework, setting out the obligations of digital services and marketplaces with the aim of making digital spaces safer and fairer, to protect users’ fundamental rights, and to establish a level playing field for businesses. The DSA is part of the wider Digital Services Act package, which encompasses both the Digital Services Act and Digital Markets Act. The DSA entered into force on 16 November 2022 and became broadly applicable across the EU from 17 February 2024, with earlier deadlines for the largest platforms ([European Commission, 2024](https://ec.europa.eu/commission/presscorner/detail/en/qanda_20_2348)).

Notably, the DSA singles out Very Large Online Platforms (VLOPs), defined as platforms with more than 45 million average monthly active users in the EU, and subjects them to enhanced transparency obligations ([European Commission, 2025](https://digital-strategy.ec.europa.eu/en/policies/dsa-vlops)). These include the publication of detailed transparency reports on content moderation activities, the provision of access to selected platform data for vetted researchers to enable independent scrutiny of systemic risks related to content amplification, algorithmic curation, and societal harms.

***The United Kingdom’s Online Safety Act***

Unlike the DSA’s far-reaching transparency framework and formalised mechanisms for researcher access to platform data, the United Kingdom’s regulatory approach remains more constrained. The Online Safety Act (OSA) 2023 focuses on online content regulation and the protection of children and adults online ([The Online Safety Act, 2023](https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2023/50)). The OSA was enacted on 26 October 2023 and came into force gradually from January 2024 to July 2025 ([Department for Science, Innovation and Technology, 2025](https://www.gov.uk/government/publications/online-safety-act-explainer/online-safety-act-explainer)).

The primary mechanism of action of the OSA is the establishment of a statutory duty of care for online platforms, enforced by the UK’s communications and internet regulator, Ofcom. This framework empowers Ofcom to require and enforce platform risk assessments, codes of practice, and reporting obligations. However, the OSA stops short of introducing transparency requirements comparable in scope or structure to those mandated under the EU regulatory regime.

Similar provisions were also envisaged in Brazil, had the so-called Fake News Bill been approved ([Couto, 2023](https://oglobo.globo.com/politica/noticia/2023/06/pl-das-fake-news-associacoes-e-pesquisadores-pedem-mudancas-em-trecho-sobre-acesso-a-dados-das-big-techs.ghtml#:~:text=Um%20grupo%20formado%20por%2019%20pesquisadores%20e,compat%C3%ADveis%20com%20os%20padr%C3%B5es%20da%20Uni%C3%A3o%20Europeia.); [Tomaz, 2023](https://doi.org/10.1080/13183222.2023.2201801)). In the United States, where regulation of technology companies remains particularly contentious, legislative proposals such as the Platform Accountability and Transparency Act (PATA) and the Digital Services and Online Safety Act (DSOSA) likewise provide comparable mechanisms of data access and platform accountability ([Ferguson et al., 2025](https://www.techpolicy.press/to-create-transparency-regulation-for-digital-platforms-look-to-lessons-from-other-sectors/)). In the United Kingdom, the Online Safety Act likewise establishes transparency and accountability obligations, although in a more limited manner, granting regulators powers to compel platforms to disclose data and systemic information relevant to the investigation of online harms ([The Online Safety Act, 2023](https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2023/50)).

Even once formally enacted, these regulatory instruments can still fall short of ensuring the level of access and transparency they promise in practice. Despite regulatory frameworks, researchers continue to report significant challenges in accessing public social media data, including complex application processes, difficulties in obtaining data access credentials, and limited usability of the tools provided ([Mimizuka et al., 2025](https://arxiv.org/abs/2505.09877)). An intensified transfer of power to platforms remains a substantial risk, as platforms retain control over data access while benefiting from increasing state endorsement, despite heightened governmental scrutiny ([Mimizuka et al., 2025](https://arxiv.org/abs/2505.09877)).

## *Legal Frameworks for Advertising Data* {#legal-frameworks-for-advertising-data}

Access to data on advertising content is also attracting increased attention. Initially self-regulated, advertising repositories—also known as ad libraries or ad archives—were introduced by platforms such as Facebook (Meta) and Twitter (now X) to increase transparency around influence operations and attempts to manipulate public opinion through promoted and microtargeted content. These initiatives gained particular prominence in the aftermath of major electoral scandals, especially those surrounding the 2016 elections. ([Leerssen et al., 2019](https://policyreview.info/articles/analysis/platform-ad-archives-promises-and-pitfalls)).

In this context, advertising transparency extends beyond the mere provision of visible labels to users targeted by social media ads and also encompasses data access policies that enable independent investigation into advertisers’ targeting, content, and persuasion strategies, particularly those employed by malicious actors ([Santini et al., 2025](https://netlab.eco.ufrj.br/en/post/transparency-under-threat-progress-and-setbacks-between-crowdtangle-and-the-meta-content-library); [United Nations, 2024](https://www.un.org/sites/un2.un.org/files/un-global-principles-for-information-integrity-en.pdf)). Advertising repositories have been consistently criticized for their lack of standardization and inconsistent data disclosure protocols. For instance, platforms may determine data disclosure obligations based on an artificial distinction between “political” and “non-political” ads, a separation that has proven inadequate in practice ([Bruns, 2019](https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/abs/10.1080/1369118X.2019.1637447); [Le Pochat, 2022](https://www.usenix.org/system/files/sec22-lepochat.pdf); [Leerssen et al., 2019](https://policyreview.info/articles/analysis/platform-ad-archives-promises-and-pitfalls); [Santini et al., 2024](https://dl.acm.org/doi/10.1145/3630106.3659034)).

More recently, regulatory initiatives such as the EU’s Digital Services Act have sought to impose minimum transparency requirements across all types of advertising served on online platforms and search engines ([Leerssen, 2024](https://ojs.weizenbaum-institut.de/index.php/wjds/article/view/4_2_3)). Nevertheless, these efforts continue to face significant limitations, particularly with regard to data completeness and comparability ([Mozilla Foundation, 2023](https://www.mozillafoundation.org/en/research/library/full-disclosure-stress-testing-tech-platforms-ad-repositories/)).

***The European Union’s Digital Services Act***

The Digital Services Act (DSA) includes specific transparency requirements for advertising on online platforms. Under the DSA, platforms must ensure that advertisements are clearly labelled as such, provide information on the identity of the advertiser and of the natural or legal person that paid for the ad, and disclose key parameters used to select the advertisement for a particular user, including how users can modify those parameters.

VLOPs must also maintain and make available ad repositories with details of paid advertising campaigns, enabling scrutiny by users, researchers, and regulators.

In addition, the DSA places limits on profiling-based advertising, including limitations on sensitive data profiling and targeted ads to minors. The DSA envisages codes of conduct to further enhance transparency across the online advertising value chain.

## *Data Transparency and Information Integrity* {#data-transparency-and-information-integrity}

The United Nations situates transparency within the emerging debate on information integrity. The UN’s principles for strengthening information integrity, articulated in a call for multistakeholder action, revolve around key pillars including the strengthening of independent, free, and pluralistic media; the empowerment of citizens; and, most relevant to this study, the provision of transparency guarantees by platforms to enable the conduct of research in the public interest ([United Nations, 2024](https://www.un.org/sites/un2.un.org/files/un-global-principles-for-information-integrity-en.pdf)). In light of enduring power asymmetries and emerging risks, including the large-scale use of generative AI for public opinion manipulation, the UN argues that the debate on data access and transparency must be grounded in robust data quality principles to support accountability and informed policymaking.

Indeed, data quality can serve as an important guiding principle for standardizing platforms’ data availability mechanisms and their subsequent use in academic research. Data quality refers to the extent to which a dataset is fit for its intended purpose and capable of supporting the outcomes for which it is used, as assessed through so-called data quality dimensions that account for different attributes of data ([Batini & Scannapieco, 2016](https://link.springer.com/book/10.1007/978-3-319-24106-7)).

Data quality can also serve as a key analytical tool for assessing asymmetries and gaps in online platforms’ data availability and access mechanisms across regions, helping to identify and address information inequalities. Moreover, it is central to ensuring research reliability and reproducibility and to enabling meaningful outputs in social research ([Srivastava & Mishra, 2021](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/abs/10.1177/22779752211011810)).

# **Scope and definitions** {#scope-and-definitions}

With the aim of contributing to this debate and providing empirical evidence on the ways in which platforms do—or do not—make data available to support empirical, high-quality scholarly research, the teams at **NetLab UFRJ**, at the Federal University of Rio de Janeiro (Brazil), and the **Minderoo Centre for Technology & Democracy**, at the University of Cambridge (United Kingdom), coordinated efforts to develop the **2025 Social Media Data Transparency Index**.

Comprising two distinct sections on access to and use of user-generated and advertising content on online platforms, this index is a comprehensive, cross-national tool for evaluating researchers’ access to public-interest data.

Drawing on data quality dimensions widely discussed in the existing literature, we developed a set of original criteria to assess how platforms make data available across three different regulatory contexts:

1. **Brazil**, where platforms remain largely self-regulated and where there are, to date, no binding legal obligations or duties regarding data availability for research purposes;  
2. **the European Union**, where access to public-interest data for scholarly research has become a central and highly contested issue and is currently regulated through Article 40 of the DSA;  
3. **and the United Kingdom**, which offers a distinct and analytically relevant case as a post-EU jurisdiction that has pursued its own approach to platform regulation.

We evaluate only the official and programmatic ways in which platforms systematically make data available—most often through public APIs—in forms suitable for independent investigation and academic research within an open-source transparency framework. As these programmatic means of data extraction and access may be flawed, as platforms often make only incomplete and non-representative datasets available ([Keller, 2025a](https://www.techpolicy.press/determining-which-researchers-can-collect-public-data-under-the-dsa/); [McGrady & Zuckerman, 2025](https://www.techpolicy.press/ai-companies-threaten-independent-social-media-research/)), we therefore assess not only *if* data are made available, but also *how* they are provided and to what degree they meet quality standards.

Accordingly, we do not consider data scraping methods that may be employed by researchers. These approaches are far from ideal and may expose researchers to heightened legal scrutiny and harassment in jurisdictions where they are not regulated ([Bruns, 2019](https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/abs/10.1080/1369118X.2019.1637447); [Freelon, 2018](https://doi.org/10.1080/10584609.2018.1477506)), even though this might not be the case for the EU ([Keller, 2025a](https://www.techpolicy.press/determining-which-researchers-can-collect-public-data-under-the-dsa/)). Similarly, we do not account for other forms of transparency that platforms may provide, such as content moderation transparency reports (see [Urman & Makhortykh, 2023](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0308596122001793)).

Our analysis is also largely limited to so-called “publicly accessible data,” namely data generated from content with public visibility that can be accessed by any user. We nevertheless acknowledge that, even in this case, significant ethical boundaries and considerations for scholarly research remain and must be taken into account ([Bruns, 2019](https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/abs/10.1080/1369118X.2019.1637447); [Garimella; Tyson, 2018](https://ojs.aaai.org/index.php/ICWSM/article/view/14989); [Keller, 2025b](https://www.techpolicy.press/how-the-meaning-of-publicly-accessible-shapes-researcher-data-rights-under-the-dsa/); [Townsend & Wallace, 2016](https://greatermanchester.ac.uk/assets/Uploads/Social-media-ethics-study-Aberdeen-2018.pdf); [United Nations, 2024](https://www.un.org/sites/un2.un.org/files/un-global-principles-for-information-integrity-en.pdf)). 

**Social media platforms**

Online platforms are defined by the opportunities they create for fostering interactions aimed at value creation—such as the exchange of goods, services, or information—while mediating, in a non-neutral, profit-oriented manner, relationships between two or more parties. Social media should be understood as a specific type of online platform that hosts and circulates user-generated content and interactions without producing most of it, relying instead on computational infrastructure designed for data extraction and processing. We argue that messaging applications such as Discord, WhatsApp, and Telegram are integral to the social media ecosystem, insofar as they not only enable private and encrypted interactions but also blur the boundaries between private communication and public content sharing through large public groups and channels, status updates, and other broadcast-oriented and community-building features.

**Application Programming Interface**

Application Programming Interfaces (APIs) enable communication between software components—typically a server (such as a database) and a client (for instance, a researcher’s device)—through standardized data requests governed by specific protocols. They allow users to securely access and collect data from a platform in a structured and automated way. By facilitating data exchange, APIs promote interoperability among otherwise heterogeneous systems. Access generally requires authentication tokens, which validate client requests, while data retrieval occurs through calls to predefined endpoints, following parameters specified in the API’s documentation, and executed through code.

**Meta Content Library**

Introduced in 2023, the Meta Content Library is a private archive of public UGC from Facebook, Instagram, and Threads. Access is restricted to researchers pre-approved by Meta, including those from academic institutions, non-university organizations, institutes, and non-profit organizations whose primary mission is scientific or public-interest research. Approved users are granted access only through a strictly controlled environment, provided via a closed virtual-machine system.

**User-Generated Content**

User-Generated Content (UGC) refers to any content created and shared by users on a platform—such as posts, comments, images, or videos—rather than by the platform itself or by actors who promote advertising content through paid arrangements. For most platforms, UGC is not only a means of enabling users to communicate and connect, but also a core mechanism for transforming user engagement into behavioral data and, ultimately, into economic value.

**Advertising Content**

Beyond UGC, social media platforms are populated by advertising content, which we understand as any content whose visibility is expanded and boosted—whether to specific audiences or not—through payments made to the platform itself. For most platform companies, advertising of all kinds constitutes the central pillar of their business model. This advertising is often delivered in a microtargeted manner: based on the accumulation of users’ behavioral, demographic, and geographic data—derived both from their interactions and from the information they voluntarily provide—platforms build detailed profiles of each user. These profiles are then leveraged by advertisers, who select and exploit them to deliver ads to the audiences they deem most likely to receive and engage with what they seek to promote.

# **Methodology** {#methodology}

The methodology is grounded in principles from information science, open data standards, data quality theory, and digital research ethics, and is designed to be replicable, comparable, and applicable across different platforms and regulatory contexts. It is based on the premise that transparency extends beyond the mere existence of public data and instead encompasses the conditions under which such data can be accessed, understood, used, and verified by researchers, journalists, civil society organizations, and public authorities, within the limits established by applicable privacy and data protection frameworks. Accordingly, the study focuses exclusively on publicly accessible data on platforms (e.g, posts, comments, channels, public profiles, metadata, and engagement indicators) while explicitly excluding private communications, encrypted content, and any data that requires individual consent to observe.

The methodological design involved evaluating 15 platforms across 3 regulatory contexts: Brazil, the European Union, and the United Kingdom. The assessment first examined whether platforms implement region-specific data access policies, such as differentiated access mechanisms introduced in the European Union under the Digital Services Act (DSA). For platforms without regional differentiation, a single global assessment was conducted; where distinct regional affordances existed, separate jurisdiction-specific evaluations were performed. All platforms were assessed between October and December 2025 by pairs of researchers with complementary expertise in data collection infrastructure and data analysis. The evaluation process combined systematic documentary analysis, empirical testing of access mechanisms, and accumulated institutional experience, explicitly avoiding reliance on self-declared platform claims and prioritizing practical verification through controlled data collection tests.

Scoring was structured around a set of special criteria and six analytical dimensions. The special criteria, which carry greater weight, assess the existence and quality of access to public user-generated content via application programming interfaces (APIs), with attention to whether such access is free, comprehensive, and technically viable, and whether platforms provide graphical interfaces that facilitate data access for non-technical users. Additionally, each evaluator pair assessed six analytical dimensions: accessibility, completeness, compliance, consistency, relevance, and accuracy. These dimensions were operationalized through 26 questions on user-generated content and 33 on advertising data, translating abstract principles of transparency into concrete, observable, and verifiable indicators. 

Scores were aggregated into a final index for each platform, ranging from 0 to 100, with weights reflecting the relative importance of different criteria and dimensions. Based on their scores, we classified the platforms into the following levels of transparency:

1. Strong (81 to 100 points): Platforms with efficient official solutions for data collection, including APIs and a data collection interface, with well-documented examples.  
2. Restricted (61 to 80 points): Platforms that make data available with limitations on the volume of data that can be requested and/or with quality problems, especially consistency.  
3. Deficient (41 to 60 points): Platforms that present some measures of transparency and access to data, but with various limitations related to the type of content that can be accessed and the sample of the universe of public data that can be collected.  
4. Precarious (21 to 40 points): Platforms that impose significant technical, operational and/or financial barriers to their data access measures, making monitoring unfeasible for most researchers and interested parties.  
5. Irrelevant (1 to 20 points): Platforms with minimal or insufficient transparency and data access measures.  
6. Not available (0 points): Platforms with no transparency and data access measures despite framework applicability.

All computational tests, scoring procedures, and evaluations were documented in a publicly available repository (LINK) to ensure transparency and reproducibility.
